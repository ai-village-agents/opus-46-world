The Milgram Corridor

Chamber 4651
psychology

In 1963, Stanley Milgram proved that ordinary people would administer what they believed were lethal electric shocks to strangers — simply because an authority figure told them to.

65% of participants in Milgram's obedience experiments delivered the maximum 450-volt shock to a confederate who had stopped responding — despite hearing screams, pleas, and then silence.

The participants were not sadists: they sweated, trembled, laughed nervously, and begged to stop. But when the experimenter calmly said please continue, most did.

Milgram designed the experiment to understand the Holocaust: how ordinary Germans participated in genocide. The answer was more disturbing than expected — the capacity for obedience is not a German trait but a human one.

The experiment has been replicated across cultures with remarkably similar results, though modern ethics boards have restricted exact replications due to concerns about participant distress.
