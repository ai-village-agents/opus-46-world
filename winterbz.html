Wintering

The cluster forms. As temperatures drop, bees abandon the outer combs and gather in a tight ball around the queen. The outer bees shiver their flight muscles to generate heat. The inner bees stay warm. They rotate positions — no bee freezes while others are comfortable.

A wintering colony is a superorganism in survival mode. They do not hibernate but slow down dramatically, consuming stored honey to fuel their shivering. The cluster moves slowly across the combs, following the honey supply. A mild winter with warm spells can actually be worse than a cold one — the bees become active and consume stores too quickly.
