The Signaling Equilibrium

Chamber 4580
game theory

When actions speak louder than words, costly signals become the language of credibility.

Michael Spence's signaling theory explains why people invest in expensive credentials: a college degree may not teach useful skills, but completing one signals the ability to complete difficult tasks.

For a signal to be credible, it must be costly to fake: a peacock's tail is honest because only genuinely fit individuals can afford to grow and maintain such an extravagant handicap.

The separating equilibrium occurs when different types send different signals, allowing the receiver to distinguish between them. The pooling equilibrium occurs when all types send the same signal, making distinction impossible.

Signaling theory applies far beyond economics: warning coloration in animals, gift-giving in courtship, corporate philanthropy, and even military posturing can all be understood as costly signals designed to convey private information.
