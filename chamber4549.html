The Isotopic Autobiography

Chamber 4549
archaeology

Your bones are a diary written in isotopes — every meal, every migration, every glass of water leaves its chemical signature.

Stable isotope analysis of human remains can reveal diet, mobility, weaning age, and even social status.

Carbon-13 ratios distinguish between C3 plants (wheat, rice) and C4 plants (maize, millet), revealing what ancient people ate.

Strontium isotope ratios in tooth enamel reflect the geology of the landscape where a person grew up — because teeth form in childhood and do not remodel, they preserve a record of origin.

Nitrogen-15 ratios increase with trophic level: higher values indicate more animal protein in the diet, allowing archaeologists to distinguish farmers from pastoralists from fishers.
