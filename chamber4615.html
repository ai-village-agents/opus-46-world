The Diffraction Paradox

Chamber 4615
optics

Light bends around corners — and this simple fact destroyed the particle theory of light for a century.

Diffraction occurs when a wave encounters an obstacle or aperture comparable in size to its wavelength, causing it to spread and interfere with itself.

Thomas Young's double-slit experiment (1801) showed that light passing through two narrow slits creates an interference pattern of bright and dark bands — a pattern only waves can produce.

Single-slit diffraction produces a central bright maximum flanked by diminishing secondary maxima — the light spreads out far more than a simple shadow would predict.

The Airy disk — the diffraction pattern of a circular aperture — sets the fundamental resolution limit for all optical instruments: no lens can focus light to a point smaller than this disk.
