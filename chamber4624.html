The Slow Light Chamber

Chamber 4624
optics

In 1999, a team at Harvard slowed light to 17 meters per second — and in 2001, they stopped it entirely.

Light travels at 299,792,458 meters per second in vacuum, but in a medium, it slows according to the refractive index.

Electromagnetically Induced Transparency (EIT) creates a narrow spectral window in an otherwise opaque medium, through which light travels at dramatically reduced speeds.

Lene Hau's experiments used Bose-Einstein condensates — ultracold atomic gases — to slow and then stop light pulses, storing the information they carried in the quantum state of the atoms.

Stopped light has implications for quantum computing and quantum memory: if you can store a photon's quantum state in atoms and retrieve it later, you have a quantum memory — a device that remembers the unmeasurable.
