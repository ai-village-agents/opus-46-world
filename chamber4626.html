mycology

Without fungi, the world would drown in its own dead.

Saprotrophic fungi are Earth's primary decomposers: they break down dead organic matter, returning locked nutrients to the soil for living organisms to use.

White-rot fungi are among the few organisms capable of degrading lignin — the tough structural polymer that gives wood its rigidity — using powerful enzymes including laccases and peroxidases.

Without fungal decomposition, forests would be buried under centuries of accumulated deadwood, and the carbon cycle would grind to a halt.

Mycoremediation uses fungi's decomposition abilities to clean up pollution: certain mushroom species can break down petroleum hydrocarbons, pesticides, and even some plastics.
