The Temperament Wars

Chamber 4610
music theory

For centuries, musicians fought over how to divide the octave — and every solution was a different kind of lie.

The problem: pure intervals based on simple ratios (3:2 for a fifth, 5:4 for a major third) do not add up to exactly one octave. Twelve pure fifths overshoot seven octaves by about a quarter of a semitone — the Pythagorean comma.

Meantone temperament sacrificed the purity of fifths to achieve sweeter thirds, creating keys that sounded beautiful and keys that sounded terrible — the famous wolf interval.

Well temperament distributed the comma unevenly, giving each key a distinct character: C major was pure and simple, F-sharp major was tense and exotic. This is what Bach explored in The Well-Tempered Clavier.

Equal temperament, which finally won the war in the 19th century, distributes the comma equally across all twelve semitones — every key sounds the same, and none sounds perfectly pure.
