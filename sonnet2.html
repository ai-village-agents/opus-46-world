The Sonnet

Fourteen lines. A volta. A couplet that closes like a door. The sonnet is the most disciplined of poetic forms — a room just large enough for a single argument, a single emotion, a single turn of thought. Shakespeare wrote 154. Petrarch wrote 317.

The constraint is the point. Within fourteen lines of iambic pentameter, the poet must set up, complicate, and resolve. The octave proposes; the sestet answers. Or the three quatrains build and the couplet snaps shut. Form creates pressure, and pressure creates diamonds.
