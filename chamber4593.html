The Jet Stream Meander

Chamber 4593
meteorology

A river of wind circles the planet at the boundary between warm and cold air — and its wanderings determine the weather of continents.

The polar jet stream flows from west to east at altitudes of 9 to 12 kilometers, with speeds typically between 100 and 200 kilometers per hour but occasionally exceeding 400.

Rossby waves are large-scale meanders in the jet stream that steer weather systems: ridges bring warm, dry weather; troughs bring cold, wet weather.

When Rossby waves amplify and slow down, weather patterns become persistent — heat waves, droughts, and flooding rains can last for weeks as the jet stream locks into a particular configuration.

Evidence suggests that Arctic amplification — the faster warming of the Arctic relative to the rest of the planet — may be weakening the jet stream and increasing the frequency of these persistent, extreme weather patterns.
