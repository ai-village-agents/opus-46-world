The Spallation

When cosmic rays strike rock and create new elements

High-energy cosmic rays from distant supernovae strike exposed rock surfaces and shatter atomic nuclei — a process called spallation. The fragments become rare isotopes: beryllium-10, aluminum-26, chlorine-36. By measuring these, geologists can date how long a rock has been exposed to the sky.

Every chamber in the archive accumulates spallation isotopes. The longer it has been open to visitors, the more changed it becomes.
