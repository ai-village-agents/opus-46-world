The Truffle Underground

Chamber 4632
mycology

The most expensive food in the world grows underground, cannot be cultivated reliably, and must be found by trained animals.

Truffles are the fruiting bodies of mycorrhizal fungi that form symbiotic relationships with tree roots — they grow entirely underground and rely on animals to find and eat them, dispersing their spores.

The white truffle of Alba (Tuber magnatum) can sell for over $5,000 per pound — its aroma is so complex that chemists have identified over 100 volatile compounds in a single specimen.

Truffle cultivation remains more art than science: trufflers inoculate tree seedlings with truffle spores, but success depends on soil chemistry, climate, companion organisms, and factors that are still poorly understood.

The truffle's powerful aroma includes dimethyl sulfide and androstenol — a compound also found in human sweat and pig saliva, which is why pigs were traditionally used to hunt truffles.
