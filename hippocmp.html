Where memories are made

The hippocampus—named for its resemblance to a seahorse—is where short-term memories are converted to long-term storage. Damage it and you can remember your childhood but not what happened five minutes ago.

London taxi drivers, who must memorize 25,000 streets, have measurably larger hippocampi than bus drivers who follow fixed routes. The brain physically grows to accommodate what it needs to remember.

The hippocampus does not store memories—it orchestrates their creation, binding together the sights, sounds, smells, and emotions of an experience into a coherent episode that can be filed away and later retrieved.
