Words as currency

Alfred Butts analyzed the front pages of newspapers to determine letter frequency, then assigned point values inversely: common letters score low, rare letters score high. The game's economy is built on the scarcity principle of language itself.

Scrabble transforms vocabulary into strategy. Knowing obscure two-letter words (qi, za, xi) is more valuable than knowing eloquent prose. The game rewards a specific kind of knowledge—not meaning but scoring potential. Words become pure currency.

Competitive Scrabble players memorize the dictionary without knowing definitions. They know that cwm scores 10 points without knowing it means a mountain hollow. The game strips words of meaning and leaves only their geometric and numerical properties.
