The Enigma Machine

Three rotors, a reflector, and a plugboard — the Enigma machine created an astronomical number of possible settings. Each keystroke advanced the rotors, meaning the same letter pressed twice produced different ciphertext. The Germans believed it unbreakable.

At Bletchley Park, Alan Turing and his colleagues broke Enigma by exploiting its one weakness: a letter could never encrypt to itself. From this tiny constraint, they built the Bombe — an electromechanical device that tested thousands of rotor settings per hour. Mathematics defeated the machine.
