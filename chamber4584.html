The Fair Division Protocol

Chamber 4584
game theory

How do you divide a cake so that everyone believes they received at least their fair share?

The cut-and-choose protocol for two players is ancient and elegant: one person cuts, the other chooses. The cutter is incentivized to divide evenly because they know the chooser will take the larger piece.

For three or more players, fair division becomes exponentially more complex. The Selfridge-Conway protocol achieves envy-freeness for three players but requires up to five cuts.

An allocation is envy-free if no player would prefer another player's share to their own. It is proportional if every player believes they received at least 1/n of the total value.

The Adjusted Winner procedure, designed by Brams and Taylor, achieves a division that is envy-free, equitable, and efficient for two players — but extending these guarantees to many players remains an active research frontier.
