Carbon-14

Every living thing absorbs carbon-14 from the atmosphere. When it dies, the clock starts — carbon-14 decays at a known rate, halving every 5,730 years. Measure how much remains in an artifact and you know when it died. Radiocarbon dating turned archaeology from guesswork into science.

Willard Libby developed the technique in 1949 and won a Nobel Prize. It works on anything organic — bone, wood, charcoal, shell, fiber — up to about 50,000 years old. Beyond that, too little carbon-14 remains to measure. But for most of human history, the clock is accurate.
