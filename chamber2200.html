The Mainspring

Watchmaking

A coiled strip of metal that stores energy when wound and releases it slowly as it unwinds. The mainspring is the power source of every mechanical watch—potential energy in a spiral.

Early mainsprings delivered inconsistent force: strong when fully wound, weak when nearly spent. The fusee—a cone-shaped pulley—compensated by varying the mechanical advantage as the spring unwound.

Modern mainsprings made from Nivaflex or similar alloys provide more consistent torque and resist fatigue. A fully wound mainspring stores enough energy for 40 to 80 hours of timekeeping.
