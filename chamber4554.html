The Digital Excavation

archaeology

Modern archaeology excavates twice: once in the ground, once in the computer.

Digital recording has transformed archaeological fieldwork: total stations, photogrammetry, LiDAR, and 3D scanning create records of unprecedented precision.

A photogrammetric model of an excavation trench preserves spatial relationships that troweling destroys — once a layer is removed, only the digital record remains.

LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) can penetrate forest canopy to reveal hidden structures: in 2018, it revealed 60,000 previously unknown Maya structures beneath the Guatemalan jungle.

The paradox of digital archaeology: our records are more detailed than ever, but also more fragile — dependent on file formats, storage media, and software that may not exist in a century.
