The fifth taste, hidden in plain sight

For centuries, Western cuisine recognized only four tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter. Then in 1908, Japanese chemist Kikunae Ikeda isolated glutamate from kelp broth and named the fifth taste: umami — "pleasant savory taste."

It took nearly a century for Western science to accept umami as a real taste. The tongue has specific receptors for it. Parmesan cheese, tomatoes, mushrooms, soy sauce, aged meat — all rich in glutamate. The taste was always there. We just didn't have a word for it.
