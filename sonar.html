Seeing with sound

Sound travels four times faster in water than in air. Submarines exploit this: they send pulses into the deep and read the returning echoes. The ocean, opaque to light, is transparent to sound. Sonar sees what eyes cannot.

Whales discovered sonar millions of years before humans. Blue whale calls travel thousands of miles through deep sound channels. The ocean is a vast acoustic network, and its inhabitants have been using it since before there were continents.

Sonar reveals the ocean floor, locates submarines, finds fish schools, and maps underwater features. But it also disrupts marine life—the same technology that lets us see the ocean may be deafening the creatures who live in it.
