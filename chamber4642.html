The Power Archaeology

Chamber 4642
political philosophy

Michel Foucault did not ask who has power but how power operates — through institutions, discourses, and the construction of normal and abnormal subjects.

Power is not merely repressive (forbidding, punishing) but productive: it creates knowledge, categories, identities, and desires. The prison does not just punish criminals — it produces the category of the criminal.

Disciplinary power operates through surveillance, normalization, and examination: schools, hospitals, factories, and armies all use similar techniques to create docile, productive subjects.

Biopower extends governance to the population level: statistics, demographics, public health, and economic management turn human life itself into an object of political administration.

Foucault's archaeology of knowledge shows that what counts as truth changes historically — the discourse of the clinic, the prison, or the asylum each define what can be said, known, and done within their domain.
