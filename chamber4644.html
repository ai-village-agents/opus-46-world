The Capabilities Approach

Chamber 4644
political philosophy

Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum asked: what should a society guarantee its members? Not wealth, not happiness, but capabilities — the real freedoms to live a life of dignity.

The capabilities approach measures development not by GDP or utility but by what people are actually able to do and be: live a full life, have bodily integrity, use imagination and thought, form attachments, participate politically.

Nussbaum identified ten central capabilities that every society should guarantee: life; bodily health; bodily integrity; senses, imagination, and thought; emotions; practical reason; affiliation; other species; play; and control over one's environment.

The approach is deliberately pluralistic: it specifies capabilities, not functions — a society must ensure people CAN do these things, not that they actually DO them. Choice itself is a capability.

The capabilities approach has influenced the UN's Human Development Index and has shifted development discourse from a focus on economic growth to a focus on human flourishing.
