mycology

A single mushroom can release billions of spores — each one a potential organism, drifting on air currents to colonize new territory.

Spore dispersal strategies are astonishingly diverse: some fungi catapult spores with water droplets, others use wind, insects, rain splash, or even animal digestion.

Pilobolus, the dung cannon fungus, launches its spore packet up to two meters at accelerations exceeding 20,000g — among the fastest movements in the natural world.

Fungal spores have been found in the upper atmosphere, in deep ocean sediments, and in Antarctic ice cores dating back millions of years — they are among the most widely distributed biological entities on Earth.

The durability of spores is remarkable: some remain viable for decades, waiting in the soil for conditions that may never come.
