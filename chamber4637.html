The Legitimate Authority

Chamber 4637
political philosophy

When is obedience to authority justified — and when does obedience become complicity?

Max Weber identified three sources of legitimate authority: tradition (the king rules because kings have always ruled), charisma (the leader rules through personal magnetism and perceived destiny), and rational-legal authority (the official rules because the law grants the position its power).

The problem of political obligation asks why citizens should obey the law at all — consent theory says we agreed (but did we?), fairness theory says we owe it to fellow citizens, and natural duty theory says we must support just institutions.

Civil disobedience — deliberately breaking unjust laws while accepting punishment — claims a space between obedience and revolution: the protester respects the law even while breaking it.

Hannah Arendt warned that the most dangerous form of obedience is thoughtless compliance — the banality of evil, where ordinary people participate in atrocities simply because they are following orders.
