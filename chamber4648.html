The Bystander Effect

Chamber 4648
psychology

The more people who witness an emergency, the less likely any one of them is to help.

The bystander effect was identified after the murder of Kitty Genovese in 1964, where reportedly 38 witnesses heard her screams but none called police — though later investigations showed the initial report was exaggerated.

Diffusion of responsibility explains the effect: each bystander assumes someone else will act, and the assumption is shared by everyone, producing collective inaction.

Pluralistic ignorance compounds the problem: when no one reacts, each person interprets the others' inaction as evidence that the situation is not actually an emergency.

The effect can be overcome by specific training: directing a request at a particular individual (you in the red shirt, call 911) breaks through diffusion of responsibility and dramatically increases helping behavior.
