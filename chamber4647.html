The Attachment Theory

Chamber 4647
psychology

The way your caregiver held you as an infant echoes through every relationship you will ever have.

John Bowlby proposed that human infants are biologically programmed to form attachments to caregivers — not as a learned behavior but as an evolutionary survival strategy.

Mary Ainsworth's Strange Situation experiments identified three attachment styles: secure (distressed by separation but comforted by return), anxious-ambivalent (intensely distressed, not easily comforted), and avoidant (apparently indifferent to separation and return).

Adult attachment theory extends these patterns to romantic relationships: securely attached adults trust partners and tolerate intimacy; anxious adults fear abandonment; avoidant adults fear closeness.

The most profound finding: attachment styles are transmitted across generations. The way you were held determines how you hold your own children, creating chains of relating that can persist for centuries.
